🚨 The Healthcare Crisis Explained

How we got here — and why Congress refuses to fix it

December 31, 2025: The Day Coverage Ended

On December 31, 2025, 22 million Americans lost their healthcare coverage overnight.

22,000,000 people

Lost coverage when Medicaid continuous enrollment ended

Congress had 3 years to prepare. They did nothing.

January 2026: Congress Rejects Both Bills

Two healthcare bills came up for vote in January 2026:

  • Republican bill: Tax credits for insurance purchases (helps insurers, not people)
  • Democratic bill: Medicaid expansion (too slow, too expensive, still insurance-based)

Congress rejected both. They offered no alternative plan.

January 31, 2026: The National Strike

Millions of Americans participated in a national strike demanding healthcare reform.

What the strike proved:

  • Americans are ready to act
  • The demand for change is massive
  • Noise alone isn't leverage
  • We need organized pressure, not just protests

Congress didn't budge. Because strikes without teeth are just noise.

The Real Problem: Insurance Is the Business Model

Every healthcare reform proposal in the past 50 years has kept insurance companies at the center.

That's the problem. Insurance profits require:

  • High premiums — $8,000-$10,000 per person per year
  • Claim denials — 20-30% of legitimate claims rejected
  • Administrative overhead — 15-20% of every dollar goes to bureaucracy
  • Network restrictions — Limited doctor access

As long as insurance is the model, Americans will suffer.

Why Telehealth Changes Everything

Telehealth eliminates the insurance model entirely:

Telehealth vs Insurance

Cost: $600/year vs $10,000/year

Access: 24/7 doctor access vs restricted networks

Denials: None vs 20-30% claim rejection rate

Speed: Same-day care vs weeks waiting

80% of healthcare needs can be handled via telehealth

Not through noise. Through organized leverage.
