1. What You Pay Now — About $10,000

The average American pays around $10,000 a year for healthcare.

That includes:

  • premiums
  • deductibles
  • copays
  • employer contributions
  • surprise bills

The whole stack. And none of it feels like healthcare anymore — it feels like a bill collector.

For a lot of families, it's even more. For most households, healthcare is the second-largest expense after housing.

For a lot of people, that's rent money.

2. What Telehealth Costs — About $600

A full year of telehealth coverage costs about $600 per person.

Telehealth handles the everyday care most people actually use:

  • routine visits
  • prescriptions
  • check-ins
  • preventive care
  • basic diagnostics

Serious care still happens in person. Same doctors. Same hospitals.

Telehealth simply handles the routine care without the insurance bureaucracy wrapped around it.

That's a phone bill. Not a mortgage payment.

3. The Difference

The math looks like this:

$10,000 – $600 = $9,400

That's a 94% drop in routine healthcare cost.

The math isn't complicated. It's straight subtraction.

That's $9,400 that stays in your pocket instead of going to an insurance company.

4. How the Expansion Works

Now here's where it gets interesting.

That $9,400 in savings can cover roughly twelve more people at $600 each.

So the first person who switches helps fund coverage for twelve more.

Those twelve create savings that help the next group. And the next. And the next.

That's called a cascade. It's the same math insurance companies use — we're just using it for people instead of profit.

Lower costs mean more people get covered. More people covered means the system grows faster.

One person helps twelve. Those twelve help the next group. And it keeps growing.

That's not theory. That's just division.

5. What Happens When Costs Drop

When healthcare stops draining your paycheck, everything else gets easier.

  • Workers can change jobs without risking their family's coverage
  • Small businesses can finally hire
  • Unions negotiate wages instead of defending benefits
  • Families stop drowning in medical debt
  • Everyday health problems stop turning into financial emergencies

When the pressure on household budgets drops, the whole economy stabilizes.

PHIERS doesn't depend on new taxes.

It works because the cost of routine care drops dramatically.

Lower cost means:

  • more people covered
  • less financial stress
  • stronger families
  • stronger communities

When families aren't drowning in healthcare bills, they can finally get ahead again.

That's not ideology. That's arithmetic.
